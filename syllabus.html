Welcome

Welcome to Introduction to R Programming. In this course, you will learn to use the R software environment for data analysis. We will use RStudio Desktop to write, run, troubleshoot, and document code as part of a reproducible workflow. You will also practice careful problem-solving, memo writing, and responsible AI use so that you can explain both what your code does and how you figured it out.

You do not need to already see yourself as a coder to succeed in this course. R programming is a learnable practice: you will build it through repeated attempts, feedback, revision, and reflection. I will hold high expectations for your work, and I believe you can meet them with steady practice and the support built into the course.

Start here: This syllabus gives the course-wide expectations. The Course Home Page is the place to check the current weekly schedule, module links, and due dates. Canvas is used for submissions, grading, announcements, and communication when Canvas access is available.

Course learning objectives

Your questions, experiences, and goals matter in this course. Data analysis is stronger when people bring different perspectives to what questions are worth asking, what assumptions need checking, and how results should be explained.

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

  1. Share a reproducible R code workflow using desktop RStudio. You will create R work that can be re-run, with clear file names, documented steps, assumptions, and source information.
  2. Confidently problem-solve R coding tasks. You will operate RStudio, write and run scripts, troubleshoot errors, keep evidence of bugs and fixes, and compare alternative approaches.
  3. Demonstrate learning skills and AI competencies in your workflow. You will use memos to document learning, confusion, error sense-making, and responsible AI use.
  4. Prepare for the next R course or applied R work. You will build toward a small end-to-end analysis workflow that connects data, code, documentation, and explanation.

How this online course works

This course is organized around a steady online rhythm: learn by doing, practice in RStudio, submit evidence of your work, revise when needed, and choose Advanced activities when they fit your goals. Core work comes first because it builds the foundation required for the course. Advanced work gives you options for deeper practice, extra challenge, peer interaction, and higher grade bundles.

Coding involves trying things that do not work yet. Error messages, confusion, and revision are expected parts of learning R, not signs that you do not belong in the course. The course is designed so that practice, feedback, and documentation help you move from “not yet” to “ready.”

🌱 Core work

Core work is required for a C or higher. Each Core sequence usually includes a Guided Activity, a Practice Space Quiz, and a Practice Space File Upload. Core work focuses on typing code, running it, checking output, fixing mistakes, and writing memos about your process.

🌳 Advanced work

Advanced work is optional, but it is how you move from the C bundle toward the B or A bundle. Advanced work may include discussions, reflections, peer review, screenshots, Git/GitHub practice, AI-related activities, or longer file submissions.

Grading: Specifications Grading

This course uses Specifications Grading. Instead of earning partial-credit points, each activity is evaluated by whether it meets the stated requirements. Your final grade is determined by the highest bundle you fully complete.

Status labels

  • ✅ Complete: the work meets the specifications.
  • 🟡 Not Ready: revision is needed. This is not a judgment of your ability; it means the work is still in progress and has a specific next step. For eligible Core work, you can revise and resubmit within the course rules.
  • ❌ Missing: required 🌱 Core work was not submitted.
  • ⭕ No Submission: on 🌳 Advanced work, this usually means you did not choose that option. It does not count against you, but it also does not count toward B or A.

Weekly rhythm

Most weeks include two Core chapter sequences. Because this is a shortened 7-week course, plan to work most days per week. Consider starting activities a day or two before the due date. The exact dates are listed on the Course Calendar.

  • Early week: start the module, complete the first Guided Activity, and begin the first Practice Space.
  • Midweek: complete the second Guided Activity, check feedback, and revise any Not Ready Core work.
  • Late week: finish Core submissions, complete selected Advanced work, and check your grade/requirement status.

Submission standards

To earn Complete, submit the correct work in the correct place and meet all specifications for that item.

First attempts do not have to be perfect; they need to be honest evidence of your current workflow so feedback can help you improve.

  • Your code should run from top to bottom when required, ideally from a clean R environment.
  • Submit the required file type, such as .R, .Rmd, .qmd, .html, or screenshot, as instructed.
  • Include required memos that explain your goal, what happened, what you changed, and what you learned.
  • Use clear file names and keep your course files organized in folders.
  • Check that uploaded files are not blank, cut off, corrupted, or from the wrong assignment.

In Core Practice Space quizzes, a score below 100% is Not Ready. You may retake Core quizzes as many times as needed before they lock. Read feedback, review the chapter, and try again.

Revision windows, 🎟️ tokens, and late work

Revision is part of learning to code. If Core work is Not Ready, use feedback to identify the next step, revise, and resubmit within the course rules.

Course policy on AI use

AI tools can support learning when they help you understand concepts, make sense of errors, or ask better questions. AI tools harm learning when they replace the work you need to practice yourself. In this course, you may use AI only in ways allowed by the assignment directions.

If you are unsure whether a use of AI is allowed, ask before submitting. Asking about the boundary will not get you in trouble; it helps protect your learning and the learning goals of the assignment.

Allowed when the assignment permits it

  • Ask for an explanation of an R concept or error message.
  • Ask for troubleshooting suggestions that you then test and document yourself.
  • Use the course-approved Introduction To R Programming Chatbot Tutor.

Not allowed

  • Do not use AI to directly answer Practice Space Quiz questions.
  • Do not submit AI-generated code as your own work.
  • Do not copy and paste code from AI into your submitted work unless an assignment explicitly allows it.
  • Do not upload private, sensitive, or restricted data to an AI tool.

Required disclosure

When you use AI, document it in the assignment memo or in the location specified by the assignment directions. Include the tool you used, what you asked, how you used the response, and whether you believe its use helped or hindered your learning. You are still responsible for understanding, typing, testing, revising, and explaining the work you submit.

Instructor AI use

I may use generative AI to help edit course materials, compose emails, or improve feedback explanations. I will not upload your submitted work into an AI tool or allow AI to determine your grade. If you have questions or concerns about my AI use, please reach out to me.

Academic integrity

All work you submit must represent your own learning, effort, and understanding. You may use course materials, your notes, and allowed help resources. You may collaborate to understand ideas, but your submitted files, code, explanations, memos, and quiz answers must be your own unless the assignment clearly says otherwise.

If you are unsure whether a form of collaboration or outside help is allowed, ask before submitting. Asking about the boundary helps protect your learning.

Academic dishonesty may include plagiarism, cheating, unauthorized collaboration, fabrication, falsification, or submitting work generated by another person or tool without permission and disclosure. Boise State students are responsible for following the Student Code of Conduct and the university's academic integrity policies.
