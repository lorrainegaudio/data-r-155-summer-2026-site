Purpose

In Part B, you move from data summaries to probability modeling. Part B focuses on using a lognormal model for 8-hour wildfire spread in High-hazard areas so you can estimate tail probability, percentile distance, local likelihood, and simulated fast-growing events. This matters because analysts often need to answer risk questions from a model, not just describe observed data. In this guided activity, you will practice using plnorm(), qlnorm(), dlnorm(), and rlnorm(), then interpret what those results mean in the context of wildfire spread.

The table below summarizes the quantile-based wildfire hazard categories derived from Idaho’s continuous USFS WHP raster and the distance distributions used for an 8-hour run. The Category column lists seven bins, from Very Low to Ultra, defined from the raster’s cumulative distribution; the WHP index itself is unitless. The Probability column is the statewide area share for each bin, expressed as a percent of valid raster area with NA excluded, and is interpreted as the chance that a random ignition falls in that category. You will use the High category values when a task asks you to model 8-hour straight-line spread distance for a High-hazard ignition.

Category Probability Mean (km) SD (km) Mean log SD log
Very Low 25 2.0 1.0 0.582 0.472
Low 25 3.0 1.0 1.046 0.325
Moderate 25 4.5 1.5 1.451 0.325
High 15 7.0 2.0 1.907 0.280
Very High 5 12.0 3.0 2.455 0.246
Extreme 4 18.0 4.0 2.866 0.220
Ultra (Top 1%) 1 28.0 6.0 3.310 0.212

The table also defines a simple fire-spread scenario using plausible values for an 8-hour straight-line distance by hazard category. These distances are not estimated from the WHP raster. They are modeling assumptions chosen for this assignment. Specifically, we assume that ignitions are equally likely anywhere within the valid WHP raster, higher WHP categories correspond to larger 8-hour straight-line spread distances, and within each category, 8-hour distances follow either a zero-truncated normal distribution with the given mean and SD or a lognormal distribution.

Guiding Questions

  • What is the difference between a tail probability, a percentile, and a density value when you use a lognormal model?
  • Why does Part B focus on the High-hazard category, and what does that let you estimate about wildfire spread risk?
  • How do plnorm(), qlnorm(), dlnorm(), and rlnorm() answer different kinds of questions about the same model?
  • What does a simulated count of fast-growing ignitions add that the exact probability alone does not show?
  • How does the histogram of High_Lognorm connect to the numeric summaries and to what you expect from a lognormal distribution?

You do not submit separate answers to these questions. Use them to focus your reading, follow-along coding, and memoed comments as you work through Part B.
