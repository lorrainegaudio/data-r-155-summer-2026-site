Purpose

This 🌳 Advanced theme asks you to build a wildfire risk report in R by moving through the same kinds of steps analysts use: data wrangling, probability modeling, simulation, peer review, and revision. Across the activities, you will organize wildfire hazard data, use distribution functions to answer risk questions, simulate many possible wildfire seasons, and turn your code and results into one revised analytical report. This theme matters because realistic R work is rarely just one function or one chart. It usually involves building a workflow, checking assumptions, interpreting uncertainty, and revising based on feedback.

This theme will be most useful for students who want a more applied, end-to-end R project rather than only short, isolated practice tasks. It fits students who are curious about risk, environmental data, simulation, or how probability functions connect to real questions. It also fits students who want extra practice explaining what their code means, not just getting code to run. These 🌳 Advanced activities are optional. You can choose this theme if it matches your interests, your goals, or the kind of challenge you want to take on in Module 7.

Why choose this theme?

  • It gives you a realistic R workflow that connects wrangling, probability, simulation, visualization, and revision in one project.
  • It helps you practice skills that matter in later coursework, research, and data analysis: building reproducible reports, interpreting uncertainty, and explaining model-based results clearly.
  • It offers a stronger challenge than Core work by asking you to make one complete report and improve it through peer feedback rather than only finishing isolated chapter tasks.

Theme Learning Goals

By the end of this theme, you should be able to build, interpret, and revise a short wildfire analysis report that uses data, probability functions, and simulation to answer a realistic question in R.

  • Organize wildfire data and model inputs: build the objects, vectors, and report structure needed for a reproducible multi-part analysis.
  • Use probability functions to answer applied questions: compute and interpret probabilities, percentiles, densities, and simulated values in a wildfire spread setting.
  • Simulate repeated wildfire seasons: use weighted category sampling and Monte Carlo methods to estimate how often fast-growing wildfire events might occur.
  • Revise analytical work based on feedback: strengthen a draft by responding to peer comments, improving explanation, and producing one final report.

Activities in This Theme

This theme includes three guided activity parts, three matching Practice Space quizzes, a peer review discussion, a reply-credit step, and one final revision submission. The guided pages help you build the work, the quizzes help you check your understanding, and the peer review plus final revision help you turn the three parts into one finished wildfire report.

  1. 🔥 Wildfire Guided Activity: Part A 🤠 Data Wrangling

    Build the first part of the report by importing data, checking structure, creating hazard categories, and summarizing statewide area shares.

    • Time estimate: 1 hour
    • You will produce: a Part A report file
    • Why it matters: Good modeling starts with a clean setup, sensible categories, and verified summary objects.
  2. 🔥 Wildfire Practice Space: Part A 🤠 Data Wrangling

    Check your understanding of the main Part A coding moves.

    • Time estimate: 30 minutes
    • You will produce: quiz answers
    • Why it matters: It helps you catch object, category, and summary mistakes before later parts depend on them.
  3. 🔥 Wildfire Guided Activity: Part B 🎲 Probability

    Use lognormal probability functions to answer wildfire spread questions in High-hazard areas.

    • Time estimate: 1 hour
    • You will produce: a Part B report file
    • Why it matters: Analysts often need to interpret modeled probabilities and percentiles, not just describe raw data.
  4. 🔥 Wildfire Practice Space: Part B 🎲 Probability

    Check whether you can use and interpret the key lognormal functions correctly.

    • Time estimate: 30 minutes
    • You will produce: quiz answers
    • Why it matters: It gives you a fast self-check before moving into the more demanding Monte Carlo work.
  5. 🔥 Wildfire Discussion: Peer Review

    Post a wildfire draft and ask for targeted help on any part of the project.

    • Time estimate: less than 30 minutes
    • You will produce: initial discussion post
    • Why it matters: Specific peer feedback can help you fix code, clarify interpretation, and improve your report before final revision.
  6. 🔥 Wildfire Peer Review Reply Posts

    Reply to peers with useful, specific revision help.

    • Time estimate: less than 30 minutes
    • You will produce: reply posts
    • Why it matters: Reviewing someone else’s draft often sharpens your own understanding of what strong code and explanation look like.
  7. 🔥 Wildfire Guided Activity: Part C 🎰 Monte Carlo

    Simulate one season and many seasons to estimate how often fast-growing fires may occur statewide.

    • Time estimate: 1 hour 30 minutes
    • You will produce: a Part C report file
    • Why it matters: Simulation lets you answer complex repeated-event questions that are hard to handle with a single exact formula.
  8. 🔥 Wildfire Practice Space: Part C 🎰 Monte Carlo

    Check your understanding of the simulation workflow and its interpretation.

    • Time estimate: 30 minutes
    • You will produce: quiz answers
    • Why it matters: It helps confirm that you understand what your simulation objects mean before you finalize the report.
  9. 🔥 Wildfire Peer Review Final Revision

    Revise your wildfire draft and submit one final combined report.

    • Time estimate: 30 minutes
    • You will produce: final revision file
    • Why it matters: Revision is where separate pieces become one coherent analytical report.

Guiding Questions and Instructions

Use these questions to decide whether this theme is a good fit for you and to stay focused as you work through the parts.

Before You Begin

  1. What do you want to get out of this theme: stronger coding practice, more applied probability, more experience with simulation, or experience revising a report?
  2. Which part of this theme seems most challenging or most worth trying, and why?

While You Work

  1. What evidence will show that each step worked: correct objects, reasonable summaries, interpretable plots, or clear memo explanations?
  2. What will you do if you get stuck: check object names, inspect output, reread the task, consult help files, or ask a focused question in peer review?

After You Finish

  1. What did this theme help you understand, practice, or produce that short chapter exercises would not have given you by themselves?
  2. What part of this theme would make you choose another applied Advanced activity in the future?
