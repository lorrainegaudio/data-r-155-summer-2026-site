Purpose

In Part A, you will do the kind of early-stage work analysts do before any serious modeling can begin: load raw data, inspect what it contains, and turn a continuous hazard measure into categories you can summarize and use later. This matters because a weak setup leads to weak analysis; if your data objects, categories, and checks are not solid now, the probability and simulation work in later parts will not hold up. Part A is worth doing carefully because it builds the foundation for the full wildfire report and gives you practice making data-wrangling decisions that analysts make all the time.

  • Fire-Assignment-PartA-StudentTemplate.qmd

Guiding Questions

  • What do I need to check first to know whether whp_raw_sample.csv loaded correctly and is usable for later analysis?
  • What does one row in this dataset represent, and which columns matter most for wildfire hazard, location, and area?
  • Why is it useful to turn a continuous whp value into ordered hazard categories instead of leaving it only as raw numbers?
  • How do quantile cut points help create categories that reflect the distribution of the data rather than arbitrary breakpoints?
  • Once categories are created, what do area totals and category probabilities tell me about the statewide hazard pattern in Idaho?

Use these questions to focus your learning. You do not submit separate answers to them.
