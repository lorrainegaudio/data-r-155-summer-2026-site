Purpose

In Part C, you move from working with one wildfire scenario to simulating many possible wildfire seasons statewide. Part C focuses on statewide simulation. You will use hazard-category weights and lognormal distance models to simulate one season, then many seasons, and estimate the probability of seeing at least 50 fast-growing fires in a season. This matters because analysts often need to estimate what could happen across many repeated events, not just describe a single case. In this guided activity, you will practice building vectors, using sample.int(), rlnorm(), and replicate(), and interpreting a simulation-based answer to a wildfire risk question.

Data and Report Template

No new external dataset required: Part C does not introduce a new external dataset. Instead, you will use the category labels, probabilities, and lognormal parameter values provided in Table 1 below and build simulation objects from those values.

  • Fire-Assignment-PartC-StudentTemplate.qmd

In this part, you are creating the simulation objects directly in R from the provided table values rather than downloading a new data file.

The table below gives the wildfire hazard categories, statewide category probabilities, and the lognormal model parameters used in Parts B and C. In Part C, you will use these values to build vectors for labels, prob, meanlog, and sdlog.

Use the values in this table exactly as written and keep the order consistent when you build your vectors. That order matters because the probabilities and lognormal parameters must stay aligned with the correct wildfire hazard categories throughout the simulation.

Guiding Questions

  • Why do the order and alignment of labels, prob, meanlog, and sdlog matter before you start simulating?
  • What is the difference between simulating one wildfire season and simulating many seasons with replicate()?
  • How does sample.int() help model the idea that ignitions can occur in different hazard categories with different probabilities?
  • What does the count of fast-growing fires tell you for one season, and what does the distribution of counts across many seasons tell you?
  • How does the histogram in Task 10 support or challenge your estimated probability of at least 50 fast-growing fires?

You do not submit separate answers to these questions. Use them to focus your reading, follow-along coding, and memoed comments as you work through Part C.
