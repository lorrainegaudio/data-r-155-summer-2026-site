Purpose

This Core Practice Space Quiz checks whether you can apply the Chapter 15 Guided Activity and practice tasks on probability simulation in base R.

It focuses on using sample(), rnorm(), and replicate() to simulate outcomes, check results with appropriate tools, and interpret whether the simulated patterns seem reasonable.

This practice matters because simulation is a practical way to study chance behavior, estimate risk, and connect R code to real questions about data and variability.

What This Practice Space Checks

  • Use sample() to simulate outcomes from a discrete set, such as repeated rolls of a 20-sided die.
  • Use rnorm() to simulate continuous values from a normal distribution with a given mean and standard deviation.
  • Apply replicate() to repeat a simulation many times and store the results as a vector for later analysis.
  • Interpret checks such as table(), mean(), any(), and sum() to judge whether simulated results look reasonable or suggest a practical conclusion.
  • Explain what simulation results imply in context, such as whether a die appears roughly fair or whether a hospital should keep some larger blankets available.

Task 1: Simulate a 20-Sided Die

💭 Scenario: Your friend shows up to your DnD game with a big 3D printed 20-sided die and claims it is fair. You decide to test it. For practice, you will simulate what 300 rolls of a fair 20-sided die can look like, then check whether the results look roughly fair overall.

🎯 Simulate 300 rolls of a loaded or fair 20-sided die using values 1:20 and store the result in big_die.

# ⚡ Type this in your Quarto file. Fill in the blanks.
big_die <- sample(________, size = ________, replace = ________)

big_die_counts <- table(big_die)

# Did you roll a 20?
round(prop.table(big_die_counts), 3)
range(big_die_counts)
mean(big_die == ________)

🗣 Comment: Does this simulated die look roughly fair? Explain using the overall pattern of counts, not just whether a 20 appeared. A fair die should have counts that are similar but not exactly equal.

Word bank: 1:20, 300, TRUE, 20, FALSE, 1:300

Task 3: Baby Blanket Simulation

💭 Scenario: A hospital is considering ordering new blankets for newborns. They want to know how many blankets they should order in each size. The hospital has data on newborn lengths, which are normally distributed with a mean of 20 inches and a standard deviation of 0.9 inches. The hospital wants to know how many babies are likely to be longer than 23 inches, which would require larger blankets.

Step 1

🎯 Simulate 100 newborn lengths in inches with mean 20.0 and standard deviation 0.9. Store the result as blanketed_babies. Use rnorm().

# ⚡ Type this in your Quarto file. Fill in the blanks.
blanketed_babies <- rnorm(________, mean = ________, sd = ________)

🗣 Comment: What do you expect the distribution of baby lengths to look like? Will the values be exactly equal? Have a normal distribution? Something else? Type your answer before working ahead. Keep whatever you typed, even if you are wrong.

Step 2

🎯 Using any(), check whether any babies in blanketed_babies are longer than 23 inches. Then count how many are over 23 inches. Do not store the result as a new object.

# ✅ Type this in your Quarto file. Fill in the blanks.
any(________)

sum(________)

🗣 Comment: Yes or No — based on this simulation, does the hospital appear to need longer blankets?

Word bank: 100, 20.0, 0.9, blanketed_babies > 23, blanketed_babies > 23, blanketed_babies < 23, blanketed_babies < 23, 23, blanketed_babies > 20.0

Task 3, Step 3: Week-to-Week Risk

🎯 Repeat Steps 1 and 2 for 1000 simulated weeks. Store a vector named weeks_too_long that records, for each simulated week, how many babies were longer than 23 inches. Hint: use replicate().
