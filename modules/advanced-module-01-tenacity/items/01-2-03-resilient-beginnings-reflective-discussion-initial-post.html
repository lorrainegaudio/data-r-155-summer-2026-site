Purpose

This discussion asks you to share with your peers a starting plan for how you want to approach the course right now. You will build from your private Resilient Beginnings reflection and choose what to share with your group about why you are taking this course, how learning R connects to your goals, which bundle path seems like the best fit for you at this point, and what you will do to make that plan realistic.

This is not just an introduction board. It is a chance to make your current plan visible, compare your thinking with other students, and learn from the different ways your peers are approaching the same course. You do not need to share every private detail from your earlier reflection. Share what helps your group understand your goals, your current plan, and the choices you are making.

Initial Post Directions

Your initial post should introduce you to your group through your current course plan. Build from your guided activity notes, but do not simply paste them. Choose the parts that best explain your goals, your bundle-path decision, and how you plan to approach the course.

  1. Open the discussion from Modules and click Reply.
  2. Write one post that addresses all 4 elements below:
    1. Why this course: Briefly introduce yourself in whatever way feels appropriate for this course, then explain why you are taking this course now and what success in it would mean for you.
    2. Why R: Explain whether learning R matters a lot, somewhat, or only a little for your current goals. Name 1 or 2 concrete ways you might actually use R in the next 1–3 years.
    3. Your course path: Share which bundle path seems to fit you best right now and explain why. Also, explain why a higher or lower path would not be the better fit for you at this point.
    4. Constraints and action: Name one likely challenge or constraint that could make your plan harder to follow through on, and explain one action you will take in the first two weeks to make your plan realistic.
  3. Use specific examples from your guided activity reflection, your goals, or your past experience when appropriate.
  4. End with one discussion-worthy question for your group.
    • Your question should invite classmates to help you think about planning, fit, bundle choice, study habits, or managing constraints.
    • Avoid very broad questions like “What do you think?” or “Is anyone else nervous?”

Good Question Examples

  • How did you decide whether the B bundle or the A bundle was worth it for your goals?
  • If your schedule is unpredictable, what helps you stay on track?
  • What made you decide that learning R matters a lot, somewhat, or only a little for your plans?
  • What is one early habit that helped you follow through on a course plan in the past?

Reply Post Directions

Your replies should help move the discussion beyond introductions. Read your classmates’ plans carefully and respond in a way that helps them think more clearly about their goals, bundle-path choice, and next steps.

  1. Reply to 2 classmates in your discussion group.
  2. Each reply must include all of the following:
    • 1. Identify one strength in your classmate’s plan, reasoning, or self-awareness.
    • 2. Respond to the classmate’s question or to a specific planning issue they raised.
    • 3. Extend the discussion by doing one of these:
      • ask a follow-up question,
      • offer one concrete suggestion or strategy, or
      • make a useful comparison to your own approach when it adds something meaningful.
  3. Minimum length: 4 to 7 sentences for each reply

Good Reply Moves

  • Point to one idea from the post that stood out to you and say why it seems strong or realistic.
  • Answer your classmate’s question in a concrete way.
  • Ask a follow-up question that helps your classmate think more carefully about fit, effort, or next steps.
  • Offer one practical suggestion, habit, or resource that could help.
  • Compare your own goals, constraints, or bundle-path decision only when it adds something useful.
  • Avoid very short replies like “I agree,” “Nice job,” or “Same here.”

Discussion Guidelines

  • Post on time so your group has time to read and respond.
  • Address the actual prompt and all required parts of the assignment.
  • Use complete sentences and a respectful, professional tone.
  • Be specific. Refer to what your peer actually wrote.
  • Write replies that help the discussion move from sharing to thinking together.
  • Share thoughtfully, but only include details you are comfortable discussing in a course group. You may describe constraints in broad terms if you prefer.
  • Follow the AI directions shown on the assignment page. Your post should still reflect your own thinking and coursework.

If your discussion is set to require an initial post first, wait until you have posted before looking for peer replies.

How You Will Be Marked

The initial post and replies may be checked separately for completion.

✅ Complete when your initial post:

  • is posted in your discussion group by the deadline
  • addresses all 4 required parts of the prompt
  • includes at least one concrete way you may use R
  • names a bundle path and explains why it fits you better than a higher or lower option right now
  • names one likely challenge or constraint and one realistic first-week action
  • ends with one specific question for your group

✅ Complete when your replies:

  • are posted by the reply deadline
  • respond to the required number of classmates
  • refer to specific ideas from your peers
  • identify one strength in the post
  • respond to the classmate’s question or planning issue
  • extend the discussion with a follow-up question, suggestion, or useful comparison

🟡 Not Ready if your work is:

  • missing, late, or posted in the wrong place
  • too brief or too vague
  • missing required parts of the prompt
  • generic, with little connection to the actual post you are replying to
  • made up of replies that do not help the discussion move forward

⭕ No Submission

No discussion post or reply post submitted.

If revision is allowed before the discussion closes, update your post or replies before the assignment locks.

Before You Post in Canvas

  • Draft your initial post before opening the discussion box.
  • Check that your initial post addresses all 4 required elements.
  • End with a specific question your group can answer.
  • After posting, return later to reply to 2 classmates.
  • Use replies to help classmates think more clearly about their plan, not just to agree with them.
